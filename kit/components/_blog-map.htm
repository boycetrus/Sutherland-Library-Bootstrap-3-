Markers have been used to indicate intervals of road distance since, it is said, around 150BC when a Roman governor divided the roads ‘into miles of near eight furlongs each, and set up pillars of stone to mark the divisions.’ A similar system was first introduced to New South Wales during the governorship of Lachlan Macquarie. On 19 September 1816, Macquarie wrote in his diary that he had contracted the convict stonemason, Edward Cureton, ‘to erect a very handsome Stone Obelisk in the Center of Macquarie Place, as an Ornament to this Part of the Town, and also for the purpose of measuring the miles from to [sic] all the interior parts of the Colony.’ Although the obelisk (designed by architect Francis Greenway) wasn’t completed until 1818, dozens of milestones were already in place soon after Macquarie’s directive. The first was positioned on the corner of George and Liverpool Streets, whilst subsequent markers were stationed out along the Parramatta, Liverpool and South Head Roads.
